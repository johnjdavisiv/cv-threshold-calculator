How to usearrow_upward

This app calculates your threshold pace, CV pace, and VO2max pace given a recent race performance. For runners doing physiologically-based training, these paces can be used to produce specific, distinct patterns of physiological response from the body.

When you run at threshold pace (a.k.a. “LT2 pace” or “T pace”), you are running at the fastest speed that creates a metabolic steady-state over time. At this pace, variables like blood lactate, heart rate, and oxygen consumption (VO2) will be stable over time (though see caveats here).

When you run at CV pace (a.k.a. “critical velocity” or “critical speed”), you are running exactly at the boundary separating metabolically sustainable and metabolically unsustainable paces. Different physiological variables will tilt towards or away from stability in an unpredictable fashion.

When you run at VO2max pace (a.k.a. “vVO2max”) you are running at the slowest speed that creates a metabolically unsustainable state over time. At this pace, variables like blood lactate, heart rate, and VO2 will rise inexorably towards their maximum tolerable limit (HRmax and VO2max, in the case of heart rate and VO2).

Many modern training systems, particularly American, British, and Norwegian-style training, are focused around workouts that produce these three distinct patterns of physiological response. Athletes and coaches associated with these physiologically-based approaches include Jack Daniels, Tom “Tinman” Schwartz, Peter and Sebastian Coe, Marius Bakken, and Jakob Ingebrigtsen.

To calculate your training paces, enter your race time (in min:sec) and race distance using the input dials. The calculator supports race distances from 800m to the 10k. I am currently working on adding support for half-marathon and marathon performance, but for the time being, you can convert your HM or marathon time to a 10k time and use that instead.

How this calculator worksarrow_upward

This calculator is based on the critical speed model, which is the gold standard in physiology for determining metabolically sustainable and metabolically unsustainable paces.

The predictions from this calculator are unique in that they explicitly account for uncertainty in your threshold, CV, and VO2max paces.

Because runners differ in their aerobic versus anaerobic orientation—sometimes called being a “fast-twitch” versus a “slow-twitch” runner—two runners at the same performance level can have different values for threshold, CV, and VO2max.

This calculator provides a “safe estimate” for each pace which is valid for 90% of runners, as well as the full uncertainty range (from the 10th to 90th percentiles).

The predictions from this calculator were developed from data on over 10,000 race performances from over 3,000 runners at the high school and college level competing at distances from 800m to the 10k (data from adults will be added in late February).

All of these runners competed at three different distances from 800m to the 5k during the same season, which enables applying the critical speed model to calculate CS, CS+, and CS-, which are the gold-standard estimates for CV, VO2max, and threshold pace, respectively. These estimates come out of considering the possible variation in the true value of a runner's critical speed, given their race performances.

Safe estimates, median estimates, and uncertainty rangesarrow_upward

This calculator defaults to a “safe estimate” of threshold pace, which is a pace that will produce a metabolic steady-state for 90% of runners.

The same approach is used for VO2max pace: it defaults to a pace that will produce a metabolically unsustainable state for 90% of runners.

A traditional threshold calculator app would just predict the average of that distribution (which is 6:08/mi). However, at 6:08/mi, any given 18:00 5k runner has a 50/50 chance of being in a metabolically unsustainable state!

If your goal in training is to produce a specific metabolic response, you want to do it in a reliable way. For that reason, by default this calculator uses a safe estimate that predicts a pace at which 90% of runners will be at a metabolic steady-state

If you want an estimate that's closer to what you'd get from a traditional calculator, like the VDOT app, Tinman's calculator, or the McMillan calculator, just use the median estimate mode instead—that will predict the 50th percentile instead.

Since CV pace is intentionally trying to be right at the 50th percentile, straddling metabolic stability and instability, the calculator returns the 50th percentile for CV pace in both modes.

Threshold, CV, and VO2max pace FAQsarrow_upward

Why is my predicted VO2max pace slower than other calculators?

Historically, “VO2max pace” or “vVO2max” (velocity at VO2max) was taken to be the final speed you would reach in a lab-based progressive treadmill test to determine VO2max.

As an example, you might start on a treadmill at 10 km/hr, with the speed increasing 1 km/hr every 2 minutes and continuing until exhaustion or until your VO2 hits a plateau.

That final speed—or the extrapolated speed based on your actual VO2max and your sub-maximal VO2/speed data—was deemed “the” vVO2max, or VO2max pace. It is that lab-based treadmill ramp speed that most vVO2max calculators are attempting to predict.

However, a modern understanding of exercise physiology shows that this approach is flawed. Any speed above your steady-state max (SSmax) will inevitably drive your VO2 up to VO2max; it's just a question of how long. At mile pace, it will take one mile; at 5k pace, it will take five kilometers.

Indeed, physiology research shows that “vVO2max” is strongly dependent on the treadmill protocol that you use.

Moreover, around half of all runners—even international-level elites—cannot sustain their VO2max for any appreciable length of time at all. Their VO2 just rises higher and higher, never reaching a plateau, until eventually they get so exhausted they need to stop.

So, in truth, there is no such thing as a unique vVO2max, and it makes no sense to argue about whether vVO2max is “6 min race pace” or “12 min race pace.”

Instead, the most reasonable definition of “VO2max pace” is the inverse of the way we defined threshold pace: VO2max pace is the slowest speed that will reliably bring your VO2 up to VO2max eventually. Or equivalently, it is the slowest speed that produces a metabolically unsustainable situation inside your body. In practice, this works to be very close to 5k pace.

Why is there so much uncertainty in threshold pace for middle-distance races?

If you turn on the uncertainty ranges, you'll notice that the uncertainty range for race performances over short distances, like 800m and even the mile, are much wider than for events like the 5k or 10k.

That'''s because the anaerobic contribution to middle-distance events can be enormous for a runner who is fast-twitch oriented (or miniscule for a runner who is slow-twitch oriented).

Take the case of the 800m. The anaerobic contribution to the 800m can be as little as 15% to as much as 35% of total energy expenditure. Clearly, two 800m runners on the low and high ends of this spectrum will have vastly different threshold paces, on account of their differences in aerobic fitness.

The wide uncertainty is one reason I prefer to train middle-distance runners using a full-spectrum percentage-based approach, in the style of Renato Canova, as opposed to physiological training systems. There's just too much individual variation that a physiology-based system does not account for.

Are threshold, CV, and VO2max pace different for men versus women?

No, not after accounting for performance level. Men and women at the same level of performance (e.g. 18:00 for 5k) have practically identical threshold, CV, and VO2max paces, at least in this dataset.

Are threshold, CV, and VO2max pace different for high school versus college runners?

No, not after accounting for performance level. To my surprise, high school and college runners do not differ significantly after accounting for performance level, again at least in this dataset.

Are these calculations valid for adult runners?

Hopefully. I will be adding data from adult (non-high school, non-college) runners to the model in late February, and then we will have a definite answer!

Why can't I use half-marathon or marathon times?

The critical speed model requires three distinct race performances in a short period of time across races lasting between around two and 20-25 minutes.

High school and college track are ideal sources for this kind of data, since athletes regularly compete in events from 800m to 5k.

At the college level, enough 10k runners also compete at 1500/3000/5000m or 800/1500/5000m to allow for critical speed calculations during the same season that they ran a 10k, and hence create a pair of (critical speed, 10k performance) for the model.

Unfortunately, it is very hard to find public data on half marathon and marathon runners who also compete often enough at short distances during a HM/marathon training cycle to create pairs of (critical speed, HM or M performance) to develop a model on.

I have a few ideas on this front, but I don't think I will be able to add HM/M capabilities until late spring or early summer of 2025. In the meantime, use your favorite race conversion tool to convert your half marathon or marathon time to a 10k time and use that instead.

Limitations and caveatsarrow_upward

The critical speed model is very powerful, and is regarded as the gold standard for estimating steady-state max. Even so, this calculator has a few limitations you should keep in mind.

The first is the fact that everyone in this dataset competed at three different distances from 800m to 5k during the same season (indoor or outdoor track).

In many cases, "true" middle-distance runners may only race at 400m–1000m indoors, and 400m & 800m outdoors.

These runners are not in the data used to develop this calculator, and as a result, there may be some selection bias: predictions will be "too aerobic" for some middle distance runners.

This calculator also only spans races that are roughly equivalent to 25:00–14:00 for 5k, so I do not recommend extrapolating far beyond that range.

The data are relatively sparse at paces slower than about 20:00 for 5k (and equivalent for other events) so I do not trust the predictions as much for slower times. This should be rectified with more data later this winter.

Lastly, the calculator currently uses data from high school and college runners, not amateur and recreational runners. Again, later this winter I will be expanding the dataset to address this limitation.
